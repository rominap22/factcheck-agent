2. The Historical Fact-Check (Triggers: Wikipedia)
This tests the agent's ability to pull structured, historical encyclopedic data to debunk absolute statements.

The Argument: "The Ford Mustang has always been exclusively powered by a V8 engine since its original launch in the 1960s. Ford never produced a four-cylinder or inline-six Mustang until the modern EcoBoost era, proving that true muscle cars never deviate from the V8."

What to watch for: A straightforward test for the search_wikipedia tool. The agent should pull the history of the Mustang and easily find that the very first 1964 models offered an inline-six, and the 1980s Fox Body models famously used four-cylinder engines.

4. The Fitness / Physiology Myth (Triggers: Web Search & Wikipedia)
This tests how the agent handles pervasive "bro-science" by cross-referencing sports science consensus.

The Argument: "Doing steady-state cardio completely ruins muscle hypertrophy. If you are on a strict weightlifting routine trying to transform your physique over a year, running or hiking will put your body into a catabolic state and actively destroy the muscle tissue you just built."

What to watch for: The agent should use a combination of tools to pull data on the "interference effect" in concurrent training, ultimately concluding that moderate cardio (like hiking) does not destroy muscle tissue if caloric intake and protein are sufficient.

This tests the agent's ability to fact-check nuanced international relations, specifically regarding AI hardware and semiconductor export controls.

The Argument: "Because of their continued economic ties and oil trade with Russia through the BRICS alliance, the United States has placed India under the exact same strict AI and semiconductor export controls as China. What to watch for: A standard, non-agentic LLM often gets confused by the dual realities of geopolitics (e.g., a country being friendly with Russia while simultaneously partnering with the US). Your MCP agent, however, should trigger the search_current_web or search_wikipedia tools to look up "US India tech relations 2026," "US semiconductor export controls," or the "iCET agreement" (initiative on Critical and Emerging Technology).
